Statics

Although any problem can be solved without the use of a fictitious force, it is sometimes easier to analyze it with this force. If an object is undergoing a constant acceleration, we can move into an accelerating reference frame by introducing a force with magnitude $ma$ that acts on every object of the system, and points antiparallel to the direction of the acceleration.

Idea 3: It is often helpful to switch to an noninertial reference frame to turn a dynamic problem into a statics problem.
First, we will provide a simple example of this in action.

Problem: A crate of mass $m$ lies on the ground of a truck with coefficient of friction $\mu$. What is the maximum acceleration the truck can undergo such that the crate does not slip?

Solution: To turn this into a statics problem, we can include the fictiuous force $-m\vec{a}$ such that both the truck and the crate are stationary. There is a friction force with magnitude $f=\mu mg$ in one direction and the fiticious force in the other. Balancing them gives: $$ a = \mu g $$ This is equally as simple to solve if we consider an inertial reference frame, but using this idea with the following will lead to very interesting solutions.

Idea 4: In a non-inertial reference frame, the vector sum of the gravitational force and the fictitious force act as the effective gravity.

If we lived our entire lives within a windowless room, how do we know if gravity exists or if the room is just an elevator that keeps accelerating upwards? The answer is that we can't. Therefore, in a noninertial reference frame, it is perfectly fine to treat $$\vec{F}_g = m\vec{g} + \vec{F}_\text{fictitious}$$ Problem: A pendulum bob hangs from a string connected to the side of a truck. The truck is accelerating forwards with an acceleration of magnitude $a$. What is the tension force and the angle the string makes with the vertical?

Solution: Let us switch to a noninertial reference frame by introduce the fictitious force $-m\vec{a}$.


If we treat the fictious force and the gravitational force as one combined effective gravity, then there are only two forces acting on the object:


Thus, the tension force must have the same magnitude as the magnitude of the effective gravity which is given by the Pythagorean theorem: $$ T = \sqrt{(mg)^2+(ma)^2} = m\sqrt{a^2+g^2} $$ And the angle the rope makes is the same angle the effective gravity makes (with the vertical) and that is given by: $$ \tan\theta = \frac{a}{g} $$
